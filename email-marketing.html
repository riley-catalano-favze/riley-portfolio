NetSuite Email Templates

FreeMarker and Conditional Logic

Inside NetSuite, email templates are powered by FreeMarker a Java-based templating language that injects dynamic variables and executes conditional logic at send time. I build templates that go far beyond simple name insertion.

Using if/else conditional blocks, templates automatically adapt based on customer attributes: which carrier shipped the order, which sales rep is assigned, what product category was purchased, or what stage the customer is in the pipeline. The result is a single template that produces dozens of personalized outputs with zero manual work per send.

I also build carrier-specific shipping notification templates at ABI Attachments automatically selecting the correct carrier branding, tracking URL format, and message content based on the shipment data passed from NetSuite at trigger time. Small-package carriers like FedEx and UPS get a tracking number and tracking link. Freight, LTL (less-than-truckload), hot shot, and flatbed shipments get different instructions entirely: freight class and BOL number instead of a tracking link, LTL delivery appointment guidance, hot shot expedited delivery windows, and flatbed unloading and inspection instructions. Nineteen-plus carriers are covered, each with the correct language for how that shipment actually arrives.

Every template includes MSO conditional comments for Outlook rendering, inline CSS for maximum email client compatibility, and table-based layouts that hold up across all major clients and devices.

FreeMarker Carrier-Specific Conditional Logic Example
<!-- Carrier and shipping-mode conditional --> <#if shipping_mode == "Small Package"> <p>Shipped via ${carrier}.</p> <p>Track: ${tracking_url}</p> <#elseif shipping_mode == "LTL Freight"> <p>Freight class ${freight_class}, BOL #${bol_number}.</p> <p>A delivery appointment will be scheduled.</p> <#elseif shipping_mode == "Hot Shot"> <p>Expedited hot shot delivery.</p> <p>Delivery window: ${eta_window}</p> <#elseif shipping_mode == "Flatbed"> <p>Flatbed delivery. Please inspect and</p> <p>sign for the shipment upon unloading.</p> <#else> <p>Your order is on its way.</p> <p>Tracking: ${tracking_number}</p> </#if> <!-- Rep assignment conditional --> <#if rep_assigned == true> <p>Your rep: ${rep_name}</p> <p><a href="${rep_calendar_url}"> Book a call </a></p> </#if>
What This Enables
One template produces small-package tracking emails, LTL freight and BOL notices, hot shot delivery windows, flatbed unloading instructions, and rep-assigned follow-ups automatically based on NetSuite data at send time. Zero manual work per send.

Lead Generation Email Funnel

Full-Funnel Email Strategy

Email is not just a send tool it is a sales infrastructure. I understand the entire buyer journey and how to use email to move contacts through each stage, convert them, and bring them back to buy again. But the funnel is not identical for every lead generation business. A service business and a product business need the same strategic stages, they just need different emails inside those stages, especially once logistics enters the picture. A consulting client needs a kickoff email. A product customer needs the correct carrier, the correct tracking format, and the correct delivery instructions for how that shipment actually arrives.

For Service-Based Lead Generation Businesses

01
Awareness and Top of Funnel
Capture new contacts through lead magnets, consultation requests, and form submissions. A welcome sequence introduces the service, builds credibility, and sets expectations for what working together looks like before any sales message appears.
02
Consideration and Nurture
Move interested contacts toward a decision with drip sequences built around service-specific objections: cost, timeline, and whether the service actually fits their situation. Deliver proof, case context, and education that positions the service as the obvious answer.
03
Conversion and Booking
Trigger conversion emails the moment a contact shows intent, like completing a consultation or requesting a proposal. Confirmation, scheduling, and proposal follow-up emails are direct and built to remove the last barrier before signing.
04
Onboarding and Delivery
No shipping, no tracking number, no carrier. What replaces logistics for a service business is a kickoff sequence: engagement start confirmation, project or service milestones, what to expect and when, and who the point of contact is at each stage of delivery.
05
Engagement and Repeat Business
Staying engaged after delivery is its own sequence, not an afterthought tacked onto onboarding. Check-ins, added-value content, and renewal or add-on service offers are built and sent as their own set, designed specifically to bring a client back for another engagement, a retainer, or a referral.
06
Customer Support
Support does not stop when the service is delivered. Emails that route a help request, confirm it was received, and set a clear expectation for when the client will hear back keep trust intact after the sale, the same way a warranty claim would for a product.
07
Re-engagement and Win-Back
Dormant leads who never converted and lapsed clients are not lost, they are an underutilized asset. Win-back sequences reopen the conversation. ABI's review campaign, which moved 52 reviews to 71 and a 3.6 to a 3.9 rating in two weeks, is a direct example of this kind of thinking.

For Product-Based Lead Generation Businesses

01
Awareness and Top of Funnel
Capture new contacts through spec sheet downloads, catalog requests, and product-page opt-ins. A welcome sequence introduces the product line and builds trust before any promotional content appears.
02
Consideration and Nurture
Move interested contacts toward a decision with product comparisons, use-case content, and rep-assigned follow-up. Address objections around fit, durability, and price before they surface, and route the contact to the right dealer or rep.
03
Conversion and Order Confirmation
Trigger conversion emails at purchase intent, like a quote request or completed order. Order confirmation is direct and specific, restating what was purchased and what happens next.
04
Logistics and Fulfillment
This is where product businesses diverge completely from service businesses. Shipping notifications have to match the shipping mode: small package carriers like FedEx and UPS get a tracking number and link, LTL freight gets freight class and BOL number, hot shot gets an expedited delivery window, and flatbed gets unloading and inspection instructions. Wrong information here damages trust fast, so every template is conditional on carrier and mode.
05
Engagement and Repeat Purchase
Getting a customer to buy again is its own sequence, sent as a deliberate set: usage tips, accessory or replacement part suggestions, and replenishment timing based on the product's typical lifespan, all aimed at the next purchase, not just the current one.
06
Customer Support and Warranty
Support emails that confirm a warranty claim or service request was received and help is coming keep the customer from feeling ignored during the wait. After the warranty period, support emails shift toward troubleshooting guides, replacement part ordering, and paid service options.
07
Re-engagement and Win-Back
Dormant customers are not lost, they are an underutilized asset. Win-back sequences remind them of the value they are missing and move them back into an active buying relationship, whether that means a new product line or simply reordering what they already know works.

Email List Segmentation

Segmentation Strategy

The funnel above only works if the right message reaches the right person at the right stage. Segmentation is the mechanism that makes that possible. Every list I build is broken down by real signals, not guesswork, so a cold lead and an active customer never receive the same email, and a service client and a product buyer never receive the same follow-up.

01
Last Activity and Engagement Recency
When someone last opened, clicked, booked a consultation, or made a purchase determines what they get next. An active lead gets a timely follow-up. A contact who has gone quiet for 30, 60, or 90 days gets a re-engagement sequence instead of a message assuming they are still warm. Recency drives urgency and tone.
02
Product or Service Purchased or Inquired About
What someone actually bought or asked about defines the follow-up content, not a generic newsletter. A specific product line gets accessory or replacement part offers. A specific service or consultation type gets renewal reminders and case content relevant to that service, not the full catalog of everything the business offers.
03
Pipeline or Funnel Stage
A brand-new lead, a nurtured contact weighing a decision, and a closed customer are three different audiences with three different jobs to be done. Segmenting by exactly where a contact sits in the funnel keeps every send matched to what that person actually needs to hear next.
04
Behavioral Signals
Page visits, link clicks, content downloads, and email engagement patterns reveal intent before someone ever fills out a form. A contact repeatedly viewing pricing gets a conversion-focused email. A contact engaging with educational content gets more nurture before any sales ask.
05
Customer History: New vs Repeat
A first-time buyer or first-time client needs onboarding and trust-building. A repeat customer or renewing client needs loyalty content, faster paths to reorder or rebook, and messaging that acknowledges the existing relationship instead of starting the pitch over from zero.
06
Source and Original Intent
How a contact entered the list, a specific lead magnet, a referral, a form on a specific page, tells you what problem they were trying to solve when they signed up. I use hidden fields and page or query parameter tracking at signup so that original intent can inform messaging months later, not just on day one.

Email Compliance and CAN-SPAM

How I Handle Consent and Compliance with Email Marketing and Automation

Most email marketers treat compliance as a box to check. I treat it as a founding principle. I over-analyze the fine print so every program I build is bulletproof because a single compliance failure can cost more than an entire year of email revenue, and more importantly, it is simply the right way to treat people.

Consent is specific, not general. If someone signs up for a monthly newsletter, they consented to exactly one thing: a newsletter, once a month. That is all they receive. Not a welcome sequence. Not a promotional blast a week later. Not re-enrollment into a drip campaign they never agreed to. What was promised at opt-in is what gets delivered nothing more.

This philosophy shapes how I build every list, every trigger, every workflow. Opt-in copy is precise about what the subscriber will receive, how often, and from whom. Segments are never cross-pollinated without explicit new consent. Send frequency is governed by what was promised, not by what the business wants to send.

The result: lower unsubscribe rates, higher trust, stronger long-term list health, and zero legal exposure. Compliance and performance are not in tension a clean, consent-respecting list almost always outperforms a bloated, over-messaged one.

Compliance also lives at the DNS level, not just the content level. I understand how SPF, DKIM, and DMARC are configured as TXT records on a sending domain, and why getting those records right is what keeps a legitimate program out of spam folders in the first place. Authentication is the technical half of compliance that most email marketers never touch.

The Standard I Hold
Monthly newsletter signup → monthly newsletter only

Consultation form → consultation confirmation only

Product purchase → transactional communication only

No cross-list enrollment without new explicit consent

No frequency increases without re-consent

No subject line deception, ever
Regulations I Build Around
CAN-SPAM Act / GDPR / CASL / Zoho Campaigns Terms / HubSpot Terms / NetSuite Email Policy / Klaviyo Terms / Mailchimp Terms / Platform-Specific Sender Requirements
Fun Fact
Under CAN-SPAM, every unsubscribe has to be tracked and honored, permanently. That means keeping a suppression list, or some other record, of everyone who has opted out, so they are never re-mailed by any list connected to that business again. It is a legal requirement, not a nice-to-have.
